Executive read

ROLF sits in the middle of the peer set on total June engagement. Instagram delivered the stronger rate on a small base. Facebook supplied most of ROLF's June posts.

Instagram is underused, not weak.

ROLF posted only 4 June Instagram items, but those posts held a median engagement rate of 0.94%. That beats the peer median. The sample is small, so the right move is controlled expansion, not a victory lap.

Facebook carries the calendar.

20 of ROLF's 24 June posts came from Facebook. The platform gives ROLF reliable distribution, but repeated service-schedule posts cap the ceiling unless they get stronger story packaging.

The peer winners show proof.

Sacred Heart, Martha's Kitchen, Sunnyvale, and Sunday Friends earned their best rates with posts that tied the service to people, partners, or a concrete local need. The strongest posts did not read like flyers.

Caption sentiment

I scored captions with a simple nonprofit-specific dictionary. “Uplifting” captures thanks, hope, partnership, and community language. “Need-focused” captures hunger, homelessness, cost pressure, and direct-need language. This is a tone read, not a psychological read.

Need language is useful when it has proof.

Posts about hunger, school supplies, or housing pressure performed best when they showed a concrete response: meals, supplies, partners, volunteers, or families reached.

Gratitude travels well.

Peer posts thanking partners, volunteers, and donors gave readers an easy emotional entry point. ROLF can use that more often without sounding promotional.

Pure information is necessary, but limited.

Service hours and schedule posts should stay in the mix. They need a second layer when the goal is reach: one number, one person, or one result.

Creative patterns

Format alone does not explain performance. Reels had the highest peer median on Instagram, but the sample is thin. Carousels gave Sacred Heart and Martha's Kitchen a repeatable way to package need, proof, and a call to act.

Turn service posts into proof posts.

Keep pantry hours and schedule posts, but add one visible outcome: meals packed, families served, volunteer shift filled, or a partner contribution. Readers need a number or a scene before they need a reminder.

Use Instagram for the best 2-3 stories each week.

ROLF's Instagram rate is good on too few posts. Start with two planned posts per week: one service proof carousel, one human or volunteer story. Add Reels only when there is motion worth seeing.

Write captions with one job.

Several ROLF captions combine bilingual copy, program context, hashtags, and logistics. Keep bilingual access, but make the first two lines do the work: need, proof, next step.

Measure shares and saves by proxy.

The public crawl sees likes, comments, and visible Facebook shares. For ROLF's own accounts, add native saves, profile visits, and link clicks before changing the calendar. Public engagement is only the outside view.

How to use this

This report should guide the first working session, not close the strategy. The next pass should add manual coding for theme, CTA, human presence, and impact packaging, then compare those choices against the public performance data.

First 30 days

Run a simple content rhythm: two Instagram posts and three Facebook posts per week. Every post gets one of three labels before it is made: service access, proof of impact, or invitation to help.

First dashboard

Track posting count, engagement rate, comments, shares, profile visits, and link clicks by post type. Review weekly, but make creative changes monthly so one noisy post does not steer the program.

First creative test

Take one pantry update and publish it three ways: a flyer-style post, a proof carousel, and a 20-second volunteer reel. Keep the CTA constant so the packaging test means something.

Data caveat

The crawl uses public metrics only. LinkedIn manual collection and native account analytics can change the channel recommendation, especially if donors or partners engage more heavily there.
